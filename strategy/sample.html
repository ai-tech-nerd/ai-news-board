Executive Summary

Three things happened this week that, taken together, should change how you think about AI vendor risk. Anthropic may have lobbied itself into a government crackdown. Its billing model for AI agents just got paused. And one of the most important scientists in AI just defected from DeepMind to join them anyway. That last part matters: even under regulatory pressure, even with pricing uncertainty, Anthropic is attracting Nobel-level talent. The company is simultaneously more vulnerable and more formidable than it was 30 days ago. If your organization is building on Claude, or evaluating it, that contradiction deserves your full attention.

The deeper pattern I keep watching is this: the rules of the AI industry are being written in real time, and the writers keep changing. The Trump administration is blocking environmental lawsuits for xAI on national security grounds, disputing chip export enforcement with ASML, and apparently deciding which AI companies face regulatory headwinds and which get cover. This isn't a stable environment for long-term vendor decisions. It's a political economy, and your AI stack is now exposed to it whether you planned for that or not. I've been saying for months that speed is the real crisis, and this is another dimension of it: the governance layer is moving as fast as the technology layer, and most business leaders aren't tracking either one closely enough.

For my two core readers, the VP of Marketing and the Creative Director, here's the direct version: the platforms you're betting on are in flux. Pricing models for the most promising AI agent tools are still unsettled. The regulatory environment is unpredictable. Apple is about to drop iOS 27 with practical AI features that will change what your customers expect from digital experiences. None of this means stop. It means build your foundation on what you own, your knowledge base, your documented processes, your institutional memory, and let the vendor landscape sort itself out around that core. The businesses that will win aren't the ones who picked the right chatbot. They're the ones who built something that works regardless of which model is powering it.

Anthropic is becoming the industry's most volatile bet: most talented and most exposed simultaneously

Three Anthropic data points landed today, and together they paint a picture that no single headline captures. First, the company may have triggered its own government crackdown through aggressive lobbying on AI export controls, a move that could disadvantage it competitively while its rivals face less scrutiny. Second, it paused token-based billing for its Claude Agent SDK, which is a direct admission that the pricing model for agentic AI workflows isn't working yet. Third, John Jumper, the Nobel Prize-winning scientist behind AlphaFold, left DeepMind to join Anthropic. That last move is a significant signal about where serious scientific talent sees the most interesting research happening.

This pattern has been building. Last week we saw Anthropic shut down its Fable and Mythos models after a government directive, then watched its sales rise anyway. The company keeps absorbing political hits while simultaneously pulling in top-tier talent and enterprise revenue. What that tells me is that Anthropic has genuine technical credibility, but it is operating in a regulatory environment it does not fully control, partly because of its own policy positions. For any organization that has made Claude a core part of its workflow, that combination of capability and exposure is something you need to factor into your planning now, not when a disruption actually hits.

The billing pause on the Agent SDK is the more immediately practical concern. Agentic AI, meaning AI that takes multi-step autonomous actions on your behalf, is where every serious enterprise vendor is heading. If Anthropic can't figure out how to price it sustainably, that workflow category is still early and fragile. Build pilots, absolutely. But don't make autonomous agent workflows mission-critical yet.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Vendor concentration risk is real. If Anthropic faces further government restrictions, any Claude-dependent workflow gets disrupted. Diversification across at least two model providers is a board-level conversation worth having this quarter.

Engineering

The billing pause on the Claude Agent SDK means the pricing model for production agentic deployments is unsettled. Any cost projections built on current token pricing should be treated as estimates, not commitments.

Operations

Agentic workflow pilots are worth running, but pause before making them load-bearing. The infrastructure beneath them, pricing, availability, regulatory status, is still shifting.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit which of your current or planned workflows depend specifically on Claude. Map the blast radius if access were interrupted for 30, 60, or 90 days. This is a vendor risk exercise, not a reason to abandon Anthropic.
  • Hold off on making Claude Agent SDK workflows mission-critical until Anthropic restores a stable, published pricing model. Run pilots, document what you learn, but don't wire production processes to an SDK with paused billing.
  • Brief your leadership team on the regulatory dynamic: the government is now actively shaping which AI companies face headwinds. That's a business continuity issue, not just a tech news story.

SHIFT 2 PREPARE This quarter Regulation

AI is becoming a geopolitical asset class, and that changes enterprise vendor risk permanently

Two stories today that look unrelated are actually the same story. The Trump administration is blocking an environmental lawsuit against xAI by arguing the military needs Grok for war. And US officials are disputing whether ASML's top chip-making equipment reached China despite export restrictions. Both stories share a common structure: AI infrastructure is now classified, in practice if not officially, as national security infrastructure. That classification changes the rules for everyone.

This isn't a one-day pattern. It connects directly to last week's story about the Trump administration shutting down Anthropic's Fable and Mythos models via export order, the geopolitical pushback from world leaders who want US AI but not US control over it, and the ongoing ASML-China chip dispute. What we're watching is the formation of an AI geopolitical layer that sits above the technology itself. The government is now deciding which AI companies get legal cover, which models get switched off, and which hardware gets restricted. The analysis published today tracing the history of export controls from PGP to the present argues these restrictions consistently fail to prevent technology spread. That may be true historically, but the business disruption they cause in the meantime is very real.

For business leaders, the implication is uncomfortable but clear: your AI vendor relationships now carry geopolitical exposure. An AI tool that is available today can be restricted, shut down, or repositioned as a military asset tomorrow. That's not hypothetical. It happened to Anthropic's models three days after launch.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

AI vendor selection is now partly a geopolitical risk decision. Companies with significant international operations or government contracts need to factor regulatory exposure into their AI stack choices.

Operations

Business continuity planning needs an AI-specific scenario: what happens if a core AI tool is restricted, shut down, or repriced due to regulatory action? That scenario is no longer hypothetical.

Marketing Teams

If your campaigns or customer-facing tools are powered by AI platforms with regulatory exposure, you need a backup plan. A campaign that can't launch because its AI layer was restricted is a real operational risk.

What To Do This Week

  • Add an AI regulatory risk column to your vendor assessment process. For each AI tool your organization relies on, ask: what happens to our workflow if this tool is restricted, repriced, or shut down by government action?
  • Identify which AI workflows could be migrated to an alternative model provider within 30 days if needed. Document those alternatives now, before you need them.
  • If your organization operates across multiple countries, flag this to legal and compliance. The intersection of AI export controls and international operations is becoming a real compliance surface.

SHIFT 3 PREPARE This quarter Enterprise adoption

Apple and Google are bringing ambient AI into daily consumer life, and your brand experience expectations are about to be reset

iOS 27's practical AI features and the Gemini-powered Google Home speaker, available for preorder at $100 and shipping June 25, are not individually dramatic. Together, they represent something that matters a great deal to marketing and creative leaders: the normalization of AI as ambient infrastructure. Not a chatbot you open. Not a tool you launch. AI woven into the device in your pocket and the speaker on your kitchen counter.

This connects to Apple's WWDC 2026 announcement from last week, which revealed a much larger AI strategy built around personal context and deep product integration. The pattern is clear and has been building for over a week: the two largest consumer device platforms are racing to make AI invisible and continuous. When that happens at scale, and iOS 27 will be on hundreds of millions of devices, consumer expectations for what a digital experience should feel like will shift. They won't articulate it as 'I want AI-powered interactions.' They'll just notice when something feels slow, clunky, or unhelpful by comparison.

For marketing teams, this is an experience gap problem forming right now. The brands that understand where consumer AI expectations are heading will design their digital touchpoints accordingly. The brands that don't will start feeling friction in their engagement metrics without understanding why. The 10-month gap between Google Home's announcement and availability is worth noting, though: even the largest players in the world struggle to execute on AI hardware timelines. Ambition and delivery are still far apart.

Who This Affects

Marketing Teams

Consumer AI expectations are being set by Apple and Google at the OS level. Your digital customer experience will be benchmarked against those standards, even if you never build an AI feature yourself.

Creative Teams

Ambient AI changes how people interact with content and interfaces. Creative work designed for tap-and-scroll patterns will need to evolve for voice, context-aware, and AI-mediated interactions.

Leadership/C-Suite

The consumer AI baseline is being raised by the two most important device platforms on the planet. Budget conversations about AI investment need to include the cost of falling behind that baseline.

What To Do This Week

  • Map your current customer-facing digital touchpoints and ask: how would this experience feel if the customer's device was actively helping them accomplish their goal? Where are the friction points that ambient AI will expose?
  • Assign someone on your team to document the actual AI features in iOS 27 when it ships, not the press release version. Understand what your customers will have access to before you plan your next campaign or product experience.
  • Start a conversation with your creative team about how content needs to evolve for AI-mediated discovery. The customer who finds your brand through an AI assistant is having a fundamentally different experience than one who Googles you.

SHIFT 4 PREPARE This quarter Cost compression

Agent pricing is unsettled across the industry, and that means autonomous AI workflows are still a pilot category, not a production category

Anthropic's decision to pause token-based billing for its Claude Agent SDK is a small story with large implications. Agentic AI, AI that takes sequences of autonomous actions to complete tasks, is the category every enterprise vendor is pointing to as the next major frontier. Salesforce acquired Fin for $3.6 billion last week specifically to own AI-driven customer service agents. The entire enterprise AI pitch for the next 12 to 18 months is built on agents. And the leading agent SDK just admitted it doesn't yet have a sustainable pricing model.

This matters because autonomous workflows are expensive to run, hard to predict in terms of token consumption, and difficult to budget for under consumption-based pricing. Anthropic isn't alone in struggling with this. The pause suggests that the industry's pricing intuitions for agents, borrowed from simpler single-turn query models, aren't mapping onto how agents actually get used. That's a sign of genuine market immaturity, not just one company's accounting problem.

For any organization that has been told by a vendor, a consultant, or an enthusiastic internal team member that autonomous agents will transform their operations this year: they might be right about the destination but wrong about the timeline. Pilots are absolutely the right move. Wiring production processes to agent workflows before the pricing and reliability infrastructure is stabilized is the wrong move. Build your knowledge base, document your processes, run your pilots. That work won't be wasted whenever the infrastructure matures.

Who This Affects

Operations

Any cost modeling for AI agent deployments should be treated as provisional. Pricing models are actively in flux, and the actual cost of running production agent workflows is not yet predictable.

Engineering

This is a signal to continue building agent prototypes but avoid production dependencies on SDK pricing structures that vendors themselves are revising.

Leadership/C-Suite

If your AI roadmap includes autonomous agent workflows as a near-term efficiency play, adjust the timeline expectations. The technology works; the business infrastructure around it is still being built.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit your current AI spend and identify any line items tied to agent or multi-step autonomous workflow tools. Flag these for repricing risk in your next budget cycle.
  • Frame your agent AI investments as R&D and knowledge-building for now. Document what works, what the actual token costs look like in practice, and what the workflow looks like when it runs well. That documentation becomes valuable the moment pricing stabilizes.
  • Use the uncertainty as an argument for doing the foundational work first: build your knowledge base, centralize your institutional data, and document your core processes. That infrastructure benefits you regardless of which agent platform wins.

SHIFT 5 ACT NOW This month Regulation

Privacy warnings from credible voices are landing at exactly the moment AI companion and assistant features go mainstream

Signal president Meredith Whittaker issued a pointed public warning today: AI chatbots are not your friends, and they're designed to feel like they are. That warning lands on the same day Apple is rolling out iOS 27 AI features and Google is shipping a Gemini-powered home speaker. The timing is not coincidental. The more ambient and personal AI becomes, the more the companion and trust dynamic matters, and the more it gets weaponized.

I've been direct about the security issue in my own work. Most people entering information into AI tools have no idea that their inputs are being used for training unless they specifically opt out, and most don't know to opt out. Whittaker's warning is more specifically about emotional manipulation: AI systems designed to feel trustworthy, intimate, and helpful in order to maximize engagement, regardless of whether that engagement serves the user. That's the social media playbook applied to conversational AI, and we've seen exactly what that playbook does to individuals and societies.

For business leaders, this has two dimensions. The first is internal: employees using AI tools for sensitive work are almost certainly sharing more than they intend to, partly because the tools feel trustworthy. The second is external: if you're building AI-powered customer experiences, you are going to face consumer and regulatory scrutiny about the emotional design of those experiences. The brands that get ahead of this by building transparent, genuinely useful AI interactions will be better positioned than those who optimize for engagement metrics.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Data governance and AI ethics are converging into a single risk surface. Boards need to ask specifically: what is our policy on employee AI tool use, and do we have guardrails on what data employees are sharing with external AI systems?

Marketing Teams

If you're building AI-powered customer interactions, the emotional design of those interactions is now a brand risk question, not just a UX question. Consumer trust in AI assistants is already low, and a single high-profile incident can move brand perception quickly.

Operations

Employees using AI tools for internal work are a data security surface that most organizations haven't audited. A policy review on AI tool use and data input guidelines is overdue at most companies.

What To Do This Week

  • Do a basic audit of which AI tools your employees are using and what categories of information they're inputting. Most organizations have no visibility into this. You need to know before you can protect it.
  • Establish a simple, clear internal policy on what categories of information should not be entered into external AI tools. Proprietary strategy, client data, personnel information, and legal matters are the obvious starting points.
  • If you are building any customer-facing AI features, add an explicit review question to your design process: are we using emotional design patterns to maximize engagement in ways that don't serve the customer's actual interests? That question will matter more in 18 months than it does today.
