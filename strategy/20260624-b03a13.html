How today's AI developments apply to your business.

Executive Summary

Three patterns collided today, and together they form a picture that should make every business leader uncomfortable. Oracle just laid off 21,000 people to fund AI infrastructure. GM laid off 1,300 workers before installing robots. TechCrunch is now keeping a running list of 2026 layoffs where companies explicitly cite AI as the reason. This is not a prediction anymore. It is a documented, accelerating reality. The displacement-to-rehiring gap I have been watching is no longer a theoretical concern: it is a balance sheet line item, and businesses are choosing it on purpose.

At the same time, OpenAI unveiled its first custom chip built by Broadcom, and MoEngage announced a bet on deploying millions of AI agents to replace traditional marketing workflows. These two stories point in the same direction: the companies serious about AI are moving to own the entire stack, from silicon to software to customer interaction. The gap between businesses that are actively building and businesses that are still evaluating is widening every week. I have been saying for months that the early adopters can barely keep up. Today's news confirms that the infrastructure layer, the one most businesses will depend on, is being locked up by a handful of players right now.

This pattern has been building for at least a week. The Groq raise, Amazon entering the chip market, SpaceX acquiring Cursor, the A24 deal with Google DeepMind: every day adds another brick to the same wall. The companies that understand what is being built are moving fast and spending big. If you are still in 'wait and see' mode, the question is not whether you will need to act. It is whether you will still have the option to act on your own terms when you finally do.

SHIFT 1 ACT NOW This quarter Workforce displacement

The Great Labor-for-Capital Trade Is Now Official Company Policy

Oracle's 21,000 layoffs and GM's 1,300 manufacturing cuts are not isolated cost-cutting events. They are documented, public examples of companies treating headcount as a funding mechanism for AI infrastructure. Oracle is taking on debt and cutting people simultaneously, essentially converting human capital into compute capital. GM laid off workers first, then installed robots. The sequence matters: these are planned transitions, not reactions.

TechCrunch's running layoff tracker makes this visible in a way that individual press releases obscure. When you aggregate the list, a pattern emerges: companies are no longer being vague about the reason for cuts. They are citing AI directly. That is new. A year ago, companies laid people off and blamed 'economic conditions.' Now they are naming AI as the cause, which means they have decided the reputational cost of honesty is lower than the reputational cost of being seen as slow on AI adoption.

This connects directly to what I have been calling the displacement-to-rehiring gap. The fear has always been that the speed of AI adoption would outpace the creation of new roles. We are now watching it happen in real time, documented, quarter by quarter. The ethical conversation about who bears responsibility for displaced workers is nowhere near as advanced as the displacement itself.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Board and investor pressure to show similar 'efficiency gains' is now backed by public comparables. If Oracle and GM are doing it, expect your investors to ask why you aren't, regardless of whether it makes sense for your business model.

Operations

Operations teams will be asked to quantify which roles can be automated and on what timeline. Without an existing audit of current workflows and tools, that conversation will be guesswork.

Marketing Teams

Marketing headcount is already under pressure. With MoEngage openly betting on millions of AI agents replacing campaign management workflows, the case for large marketing teams becomes harder to make to leadership without a clear articulation of where human judgment adds irreplaceable value.

What To Do This Week

  • Before leadership asks you to model headcount reductions, audit your current team's workflows to understand which tasks are repetitive and rule-based versus which require genuine human judgment, contextual knowledge, or relationship management. You want to lead this conversation, not react to it.
  • Identify two or three roles in your department where AI augmentation could increase output without requiring elimination. Propose augmentation before someone else proposes elimination. The framing matters enormously.
  • If you manage people, start having honest conversations now about skill development in AI oversight and prompt engineering. The employees who understand how to direct AI systems will be the ones with options when the next round of cuts is framed as 'efficiency improvements.'

SHIFT 2 PREPARE This quarter Consolidation

The Infrastructure Stack Is Being Locked Up, and Most Businesses Will Never Own Any of It

OpenAI's first custom chip, built by Broadcom, is a signal most business leaders will underestimate. On the surface it looks like a tech story about semiconductors. Underneath, it is about vertical integration and long-term cost control. OpenAI's dependence on Nvidia has been an existential cost risk. By designing its own silicon, OpenAI joins Google, Amazon, and Apple in the club of companies that own their compute destiny. Nvidia's dominance, while still enormous, now has multiple credible challengers being funded by the companies that were once its best customers.

This matters for businesses that are not in the chip business because it determines who controls pricing, access, and capability at the foundation layer. The companies building the infrastructure are also building the AI products that sit on top of it. That vertical integration creates structural advantages that will compound over years. A business using OpenAI, Google, or Amazon AI tools is renting from a landlord who is also your competitor's supplier, your industry's regulator, and now your chip manufacturer.

Looking back at this past week: Groq confirmed a $650M raise after the Nvidia deal, Amazon announced plans to sell its own AI chips, and now OpenAI enters the silicon game. Three stories in eight days, all pointing at the same thing. The hardware layer of AI is becoming as contested as the model layer. Businesses that have not thought about which infrastructure relationships they are building, and which dependencies they are creating, are making a long-term decision by default.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

AI vendor selection is now an infrastructure decision, not just a software procurement decision. The platform you standardize on today creates switching costs and dependencies that will be difficult and expensive to unwind in 18 to 24 months.

Engineering

Engineering teams evaluating AI infrastructure need to factor in the long-term roadmap of chip and compute providers, not just current API pricing. The economics of AI workloads will shift as custom silicon matures.

Operations

Procurement and vendor management processes built for software subscriptions are not equipped to evaluate AI infrastructure relationships. This needs dedicated attention before contracts are signed at scale.

What To Do This Week

  • Map your current AI vendor dependencies: which models, which APIs, which cloud providers. Understand where you have concentration risk, meaning where a single vendor price change or policy shift would force an expensive migration.
  • Before signing multi-year AI infrastructure contracts, ask vendors directly about their chip and compute roadmap. The answer will tell you how seriously they have thought about their own cost structure, which directly affects yours.
  • Add AI infrastructure to your annual technology audit. This is not a one-time evaluation. The hardware and model layer is moving fast enough that a vendor relationship that makes sense today may look very different in 12 months.

SHIFT 3 PREPARE This quarter Enterprise adoption

AI Agents Are Moving From Experiment to Default Workflow, Starting With High-Volume Decisions

Fika Jobs just raised $4 million to have AI agents conduct job interviews. MoEngage is deploying millions of AI agents to run marketing campaigns. AI coding agents are now directing robot training autonomously, without human-written code. These three stories share a structure: the AI agent is not assisting a human decision, it is making the first call, or in MoEngage's case, making millions of calls simultaneously with no human in the loop at the individual interaction level.

This is a meaningful line to cross. The human-in-the-loop conversation I keep returning to assumes a human is reviewing output before it reaches the end point: a candidate, a customer, a physical robot. In agent-first deployments at scale, that review layer is being compressed or eliminated entirely. The human sets the parameters and reviews aggregate performance, but individual decisions happen at machine speed. For marketing specifically, this means a VP of Marketing might set campaign objectives and review weekly metrics while AI agents make thousands of micro-decisions about messaging, timing, and targeting every hour.

The strategic question is not whether to use agents. It is whether your team has built the knowledge infrastructure, the structured context, the documented preferences, the historical performance data, that agents need to make decisions you would actually endorse. Agents given poor inputs make poor decisions at scale. The MoEngage model only works if the underlying customer data and brand guidelines are centralized and structured. Most marketing organizations I have seen are nowhere near that foundation.

Who This Affects

Marketing Teams

Agent-driven marketing platforms will be pitched to your leadership within the next two quarters. Without a structured knowledge base of brand guidelines, past campaign performance, and customer segmentation data, adopting these tools will produce volume without quality.

Creative Teams

When agents handle campaign execution at scale, the creative team's role shifts to setting the parameters, establishing the guardrails, and auditing output quality. Teams that understand how to brief AI systems will have more leverage, not less.

Operations

Hiring workflows are the immediate operational impact. AI interview agents conducting first-round screening will reach HR departments this year. Understand what that means for candidate experience, legal liability, and bias risk before it arrives.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit your marketing data before evaluating any agent-driven platform. Ask: where is our customer data? Where are our brand guidelines? Where is historical campaign performance data? If the answer is 'scattered across different tools and drives,' that is the first problem to solve, not the agent platform itself.
  • For hiring teams, evaluate the legal and ethical landscape of AI-conducted interviews before a vendor presents it as a turnkey solution. Bias in automated screening is a documented, litigated risk. You need a position on this before you are in a contract.
  • Define what 'human in the loop' means for your organization at the agent level. Not every individual decision needs human review. But you need to know which decisions do, and build that into any agent deployment from the start, not as an afterthought.

SHIFT 4 PREPARE This quarter Regulation

Policy Advocacy Around AI Has Become a Competitive Weapon, and Safety Language Is Not Safe

The Anthropic story today is one of the most strategically interesting things I have read in weeks. Ars Technica reports that Anthropic's own public safety arguments, the very positions the company has used to differentiate itself as the responsible AI lab, may have been used by the US government to justify export restrictions against it. The safety-focused rhetoric designed to build trust became the legal foundation for a regulatory action that hurts the company's business.

This is not an accident or a fluke. It is a preview of how AI policy is going to work. Governments are not neutral arbiters carefully weighing arguments. They are actors with their own interests, and any argument made publicly, regardless of its intent, is available to be repurposed. This connects to last week's story about the Trump administration's moves against Anthropic, and the broader pattern of export control debates that have been running for days. The AI policy environment is adversarial, and companies that engage in it are playing a game with rules that change mid-match.

For business leaders, the implication is not to follow Anthropic's policy battles closely. It is to recognize that the regulatory environment around AI is unstable in ways that can affect your vendor relationships overnight. A platform you have standardized on could face export restrictions, regulatory penalties, or compliance requirements that cascade down to you. That is a supply chain risk, and it deserves the same attention as any other vendor dependency.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

AI vendor risk is now regulatory risk. The companies whose tools you depend on are operating in an increasingly adversarial policy environment. Your risk assessment needs to include what happens to your operations if a key AI vendor faces restrictions or penalties.

Operations

Vendor contracts for AI tools should include regulatory change clauses. If a vendor faces export restrictions or compliance mandates that change service terms, you need to know what your options and obligations are.

What To Do This Week

  • Review your top three AI vendor relationships and ask: what happens to our operations if this vendor faces a regulatory action, export restriction, or major policy change? If the answer is 'we would have a serious problem,' that is a concentration risk worth addressing now.
  • Add regulatory status to your AI vendor review cadence. This does not require a legal team. It requires someone reading the news on your behalf and flagging when a vendor you depend on enters contested regulatory territory.
  • Diversify AI tool dependencies where it is practical. Relying on a single vendor for a critical workflow is the same risk it has always been, amplified by the fact that AI vendors are operating in an unusually volatile policy environment.

SHIFT 5 ACT NOW This month Open source vs closed

AI Reliability Is Developing a Structural Problem That Few Businesses Are Watching

Two stories today belong together, and neither is getting the attention they deserve in the context of business operations. The first is TechCrunch's analysis of feedback loops in AI development: AI systems training on AI-generated data, AI companies evaluating each other's outputs, circular dependencies creating systemic reliability risks. The second is the expert consensus piece arguing that dangerous AI models are coming regardless of safety measures.

Separately, these read as think pieces. Together, they describe a reliability landscape that should concern any business building critical workflows on AI outputs. If the training data for future models increasingly includes AI-generated content, the errors, biases, and hallucinations in current models get baked into future models. The signal degrades over time in ways that are difficult to detect until something goes wrong at scale.

I want to be direct about something here: most businesses I talk to have essentially stopped doing due diligence on AI outputs. The tools are so often right, and so impressively confident, that the human review step gets skipped. That is exactly the security and reliability risk I keep raising. AI complacency, the state where you stop checking because it is usually correct, is how you end up with an error that is accurate but contextually wrong, or a decision made on hallucinated data that no one caught because no one was looking. The feedback loop problem makes this worse, not better, over time.

Who This Affects

Marketing Teams

AI-generated content used in campaigns, especially at the scale MoEngage and similar platforms enable, needs a quality and accuracy review process that does not get compressed out of existence by speed pressure.

Creative Teams

Creative directors approving AI-assisted work at volume need a systematic review process, not spot checks. The 'more at-bats' advantage only works if someone is still calling balls and strikes.

Leadership/C-Suite

Decisions made using AI-generated research, summaries, or analysis need a verification standard. The confidence of AI output does not correlate with its accuracy, and the gap between the two is where business risk lives.

What To Do This Week

  • Establish a written standard for AI output review in your organization: which types of AI-generated content or analysis require human verification before use, and what that verification looks like. This does not need to be complex. It needs to exist.
  • Identify your highest-stakes AI-assisted workflows, the ones where an error would cause real damage, and add a mandatory human review step if one does not exist. The review step is not bureaucracy. It is the part where you catch what the AI got subtly wrong.
  • Brief your team on the feedback loop risk in plain language: the AI tools you use today are being trained on outputs that include AI-generated content, which means error patterns can compound. That is not a reason to stop using AI. It is a reason to keep the human review layer.

AI-conducted first-round interviews are arriving in enterprise hiring this year. Whether you adopt this or compete against it for talent, your recruiting team needs a position on it now, particularly on bias risk, candidate experience, and legal exposure, before a vendor pitches it as a solved problem.

OpenAI Open Source Bug Patcher

Most businesses run software that depends on open source components, and most of those businesses have no systematic way to monitor those dependencies for security vulnerabilities. OpenAI entering this space means automated security patching is about to become a standard expectation. If your engineering team is doing this manually or not at all, this signals where the floor is moving.

AI Coding Agents for Robot Training

This one is for leaders thinking beyond the current AI cycle. Coding agents that autonomously direct physical robot training close a loop that has been open since robotics and AI were separate disciplines. For any business in manufacturing, logistics, or physical operations, the timeline on autonomous physical automation just got shorter.
