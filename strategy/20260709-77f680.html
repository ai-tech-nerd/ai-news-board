How today's AI developments apply to your business.

Executive Summary

Something the numbers today won't let me ignore: the AI ecosystem is quietly reorganizing itself around control, and most businesses are watching it happen without realizing they're affected. Microsoft replacing OpenAI and Anthropic models in Excel and Outlook with its own MAI models isn't just a cost story. It's a signal that the platform layer and the model layer are separating, and whoever owns the platform controls what users actually experience. At the same time, China is reportedly moving to restrict exports of its best models, Qwen, Doubao, GLM-5.2, mirroring Washington's own export controls. Two of the world's largest economies are simultaneously pulling their AI capabilities inward. The open global AI marketplace that many businesses have been quietly depending on is starting to close.

The security number from DigiCert is the one I keep returning to: 50% of enterprises surveyed experienced at least one security incident caused by an unauthorized or misconfigured AI agent in the past six months. Not a theoretical risk. Not a future concern. A present-tense failure happening inside organizations that thought they had this under control. This connects directly to something I've been saying for months: most businesses adopted AI tools before they understood what they were running, who had access, or what those tools were sending back. That pattern is now producing real incidents. And Cloudflare's data showing that over 50% of internet traffic today is non-human, up 1,700% in AI agent requests year over year, tells you the exposure is only growing.

If you're a VP of Marketing or a Creative Director trying to figure out what to do with all of this: the single most useful thing you can do this week is not adopt a new tool. It's find out what tools are already running in your organization, who authorized them, and whether any of them are AI agents operating without proper configuration. That audit conversation is more valuable right now than any new capability you could add.

Strategic Shifts · Jul 09

SHIFT 1 ACT NOW This quarter Consolidation

Platform owners are cutting out the model middlemen, and your AI stack is about to get less predictable

Microsoft's decision to replace OpenAI and Anthropic models inside Excel and Outlook with its own MAI models is described publicly as a cost move. That's accurate, but incomplete. Tens of thousands of AI prompts run weekly through those applications now route to Microsoft-built models that most users don't know exist and can't evaluate independently. Microsoft AI Chief Mustafa Suleiman telegraphed this direction explicitly: reduce dependence on third-party models. That's not a budget optimization. That's a strategic repositioning.

This matters because businesses that built workflows, integrations, or expectations around specific model behavior inside Microsoft's ecosystem may not notice the switch until something performs differently. The model you tested against last quarter may not be the model running today. Meanwhile, Meta's Muse Image landing at #2 on Arena's text-to-image leaderboard, behind only GPT Image 2, shows another platform giant bringing image generation in-house and distributing it across Instagram, WhatsApp, Facebook, and Messenger at scale. Meta also previewed Muse Video at #3 on the video leaderboard. Two of the largest platforms in the world are now betting on proprietary models rather than licensed ones. This pattern has been building: last week's context showed Microsoft cutting AI costs with in-house models as a broader industry trend. Today confirms it's accelerating, not stabilizing.

For business leaders, this creates a real evaluation problem. The AI tools your teams are using inside enterprise platforms may be changing underneath you, and no announcement will accompany the change. The case for auditing what's actually running in your stack, and testing outputs against baseline benchmarks, has never been more practical.

Who This Affects

Operations

Workflow outputs inside Microsoft 365 tools may shift as MAI models replace OpenAI/Anthropic models. Teams relying on consistent AI-assisted output quality in Excel or Outlook need to establish performance benchmarks now so they can detect drift.

Leadership/C-Suite

Vendor dependency risk is real but often invisible. When the model layer is bundled inside platforms you're already paying for, there's no obvious signal when it changes. Demand transparency from your platform vendors on which models are powering which features.

Marketing Teams

Meta distributing Muse Image and soon Muse Video across Instagram and WhatsApp means AI-generated visual content from competitors will become ubiquitous and free. The differentiation pressure on creative quality increases, not decreases.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit every enterprise platform your team uses that has AI features, including Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, and Meta tools. Document which model is powering each feature where that information is available, and flag where it is unknown.
  • Establish output benchmarks for your most AI-dependent workflows before the end of this quarter. If Microsoft or another platform swaps models, you need a before-and-after comparison point.
  • Brief leadership on the model-inside-platform risk: the AI your organization depends on may be changing without notice or consent, and that has compliance, quality, and performance implications.

SHIFT 2 ACT NOW This month Enterprise adoption

The AI agent security crisis is no longer hypothetical: half of enterprises have already been hit

DigiCert surveyed 1,001 IT and cybersecurity leaders across three countries and found that nearly 80% of enterprises encountered AI-related security issues in the past six months. More specifically, 50% experienced at least one incident caused by an unauthorized or misconfigured AI agent. That is not a trailing indicator of risk. That is a current-state reality for the majority of organizations actively using AI.

The Cloudflare data adds critical context. AI agent requests grew 1,700% from 2025 to 2026. Over 50% of internet traffic is now non-human. Cloudflare's response was to launch a Monetization Gateway that lets site owners put resources behind paywalls for AI agents, a product announcement that got over 4 million views because the problem it's solving is urgent and widely felt. The combination of explosive agent growth and widespread security failures is not coincidental. Organizations adopted agents faster than they built governance around them. That gap is now producing incidents.

I've written before that security is the silent risk in AI adoption, and that most people are unaware that everything entered into an AI system may be used for training unless they explicitly configure otherwise. The DigiCert numbers are the empirical confirmation of what was previously a warning. Last week's context included a story about hackers exploiting nine popular AI tools to build botnets, and a first-of-its-kind AI-orchestrated ransomware attack. The threat surface is not theoretical and it is growing faster than organizational awareness.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

A 50% enterprise incident rate from AI agents is a board-level risk disclosure item. If your organization is using AI agents, and most are, this needs to be on the risk register with a clear owner and a documented governance policy.

Operations

Unauthorized or misconfigured AI agents are the specific failure mode driving incidents. Operations teams need a current inventory of every AI agent running in the business, including tools that may have been adopted by individual departments without central authorization.

Engineering

Misconfiguration is the primary vector. Engineering and IT need to establish configuration standards for AI agents, including data access permissions, output logging, and explicit opt-out settings for training data contribution.

What To Do This Week

  • Run a full AI agent inventory across every department this month. Ask each team lead: what AI tools are running in your workflow, who authorized them, and do you know what data they have access to? You will find things no one approved.
  • For every AI agent or tool identified, verify three things: whether training data contribution is turned off, what data the tool can access, and whether there is a human reviewer in the loop for consequential outputs.
  • Escalate the DigiCert findings to your CISO or equivalent and request a formal AI security review. If that role does not exist, this is the moment to assign AI security ownership to someone specific.

SHIFT 3 PREPARE This quarter Regulation

The global AI model market is fracturing along geopolitical lines, and that affects your vendor options

Beijing is reportedly discussing rules that would restrict foreign access to China's most powerful AI models: Qwen from Alibaba, Doubao from ByteDance, and GLM-5.2 from Zhipu AI. These are not fringe models. They gained real global adoption because of strong performance at lower cost than Western alternatives. If those restrictions move forward, a meaningful portion of the cost-competitive AI model market narrows or disappears for non-Chinese businesses.

This mirrors Washington's own export controls, which have already restricted access to models including Anthropic's Fable and Mythos. Two of the world's largest AI ecosystems are simultaneously pulling inward. The implication is not just geopolitical. It's commercial. Businesses that have built cost models, integrations, or workflows around globally available models need to account for the possibility that access terms change based on where those models are developed.

Combined with Microsoft pulling away from OpenAI and Anthropic to reduce licensing costs, the pattern is clear: the model layer is getting more expensive, more restricted, and more politicized. The businesses most exposed are those who adopted models quickly without evaluating alternative sources or building any redundancy. The audit-before-adopt principle applies here in a specific way: if you don't know which models you're currently running and where they originate, you have no way to assess your exposure to either geopolitical restriction or vendor pricing power.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Geopolitical risk is now a direct AI vendor risk. Organizations using models that originate from jurisdictions subject to export controls need legal and operational clarity on continuity of access.

Engineering

If your stack includes Chinese-origin models for cost reasons, build contingency integrations now while access is still open. Waiting until restrictions are formal means emergency migrations under pressure.

Operations

Cost models built around globally competitive AI pricing may not hold. Begin modeling scenarios where your current model access costs 20-40% more, or disappears, and identify which workflows are most exposed.

What To Do This Week

  • Map the country of origin for every AI model in your current stack. This is a 30-minute exercise that most organizations have never done, and it's now a material risk management question.
  • Identify which workflows are single-model dependent and which have fallback options. Prioritize building redundancy for the workflows most critical to revenue or operations.
  • Brief your legal team on the current state of AI export controls in both the US and China, and ask them to monitor the Beijing discussions for any formal rulemaking.

SHIFT 4 PREPARE This quarter Workforce displacement

The workforce displacement story is getting harder for companies to narrate around

Microsoft cut 4,800 jobs, 2.1% of its workforce, while simultaneously expanding its AI division and announcing it replaced third-party AI models with in-house ones to reduce costs. Chief People Officer Amy Coleman stated publicly that the cuts are not due to AI replacing those roles. That statement is becoming increasingly difficult to sustain as a credible narrative. The timing, the operational context, and the pattern across the industry make the connection impossible to ignore.

Last week's historical context showed Microsoft laying off nearly 5,000 additional employees, citing AI, spanning Xbox and commercial sales teams. That means Microsoft has now eliminated close to 10,000 positions in consecutive weeks while expanding its AI infrastructure. The company is not unique. The broader pattern across today's stories, from Meta building in-house models to Samsung's chip profits reflecting the infrastructure investment boom, shows capital flowing heavily toward AI capability and away from human labor at scale.

I want to be honest about what I think is actually happening here, because the displacement-to-rehiring gap is real and the pace is the problem. Historical technology displacement followed a curve: disruption, plateau, adaptation, new job creation. AI is compressing that curve in ways that don't leave time for the middle steps. The new roles being created inside companies like Microsoft require different skills than the ones being eliminated. That gap is not a transition. For many workers, it's a wall. Business leaders who frame this only as efficiency gain are missing a cultural and operational risk: the people still in the organization are watching, and the pressure that creates affects performance, trust, and retention.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

The workforce narrative matters. Organizations eliminating roles while expanding AI investment face internal trust erosion and external reputation risk. A clear, honest communication strategy around AI and workforce impact is not optional.

Marketing Teams

Marketing teams are watching these cuts in adjacent industries and wondering who is next. Clarity from leadership about where AI is augmenting versus replacing within your organization directly affects team morale and creative output.

Operations

Efficiency gains from AI should be translating to workload relief, not just headcount reduction targets. If AI is making your team more productive without reducing pressure on remaining employees, you are creating a retention problem.

What To Do This Week

  • Develop an honest internal position on AI and workforce impact in your organization: where AI is augmenting existing roles, where it is changing role scope, and where it may affect headcount. Employees need clarity, not corporate language.
  • Review whether AI efficiency gains in your organization are translating to workload relief for teams or simply being absorbed as additional output expectations. The former builds loyalty. The latter builds burnout.
  • Identify which roles in your organization are most exposed to AI-driven scope change in the next 12 months and create development pathways for those employees before the change is upon them.

SHIFT 5 ACT NOW This month Enterprise adoption

Agentic AI is becoming infrastructure, and most organizations are not governed for it

Anthropic's Claude Cowork upgrade, now cross-device with scheduled tasks that run while your computer is off, represents a meaningful step in how agents are being positioned. This is not a chatbot you interact with. It's a background process that executes tasks autonomously across sessions and devices. Anthropic's own usage data from 1.2 million anonymized sessions found that business operations is the top use case at 33.4%. Agents are not a future experiment for enterprise users. They are a current operational tool.

The Cloudflare Monetization Gateway announcement, driven by a 1,700% jump in AI agent requests year over year, confirms the scale. More than half of today's internet traffic is non-human. That is not a statistic about the future. It describes today's internet. And the DigiCert finding that 50% of enterprises have already experienced security incidents from unauthorized or misconfigured agents completes the picture: agentic AI is scaling faster than governance frameworks, and the incidents are already happening.

DoorDash publishing a detailed public grading report on AI code review performance is a small but telling signal. Enterprises are starting to evaluate AI systems the same way they evaluate vendors: with structured assessment criteria, documented performance data, and formal review processes. That is the right instinct. The organizations that will navigate the agent era well are the ones that treat AI agents as infrastructure requiring governance, not as productivity tools requiring a credit card.

Who This Affects

Operations

Agents running scheduled tasks outside of human oversight sessions require explicit governance policies: what can they access, what actions can they take autonomously, and who reviews outputs before they become consequential.

Engineering

DoorDash's approach of formally grading AI performance on specific tasks is a model worth adopting. Engineering teams need structured evaluation frameworks for AI agents before deploying them in production workflows.

Leadership/C-Suite

If more than half of internet traffic is already non-human and your organization is using any AI agents, you are participating in infrastructure that requires governance at the CIO level, not just the team lead level.

What To Do This Week

  • Establish a formal definition of what an AI agent means in your organization, distinct from a standard AI tool, and require that all agents be registered with IT before deployment. The DigiCert data shows the cost of skipping this step.
  • For any agent currently running scheduled or autonomous tasks, document: what data it accesses, what actions it can take without human approval, and who is notified when it completes a task. If you can't answer those questions, the agent is misconfigured.
  • Adopt the DoorDash model and build a structured performance evaluation for your most-used AI systems this quarter. Treat AI evaluation the same way you'd treat a vendor review: with criteria, measurement, and a documented pass/fail threshold.
