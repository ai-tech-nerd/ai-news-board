How today's AI developments apply to your business.

Executive Summary

Two things are happening simultaneously right now, and the tension between them is the story. On one side, AI infrastructure is being treated as national security: the US government is fast-tracking AI data centers to the power grid, the Trump administration is shielding xAI from environmental law by citing military necessity, and Baseten is reportedly raising another $1.5 billion just months after its last mega-round. On the other side, the political backlash is reaching a scale that cannot be ignored. Bernie Sanders is proposing $7 trillion in public AI control, and a new study from earlier this week showed only 16% of Americans believe AI will have a positive societal impact. These two forces are on a collision course, and the businesses caught in the middle are the ones that will feel it most.

What this means practically: the window for quiet, low-friction AI adoption is closing. When AI becomes a political flashpoint at the $7 trillion proposal level, regulatory volatility becomes a real operational risk. I have been saying for months that businesses cannot wait for regulation to settle before acting, and today's news reinforces that. The companies building internal AI capability now, on their own terms, with their own guardrails, are the ones that will navigate the coming turbulence. The ones waiting for clarity will inherit chaos.

There is also a structural signal buried in today's stories that I want to flag directly for creative and marketing leaders. Snap spinning its AI video team out into an independent company called Dotmo because the costs were unsustainable is not a minor footnote. It is a warning about what frontier AI development actually costs when you try to build rather than buy. Meanwhile, Anthropic just paused token-based billing for its Agent SDK because the pricing model was creating unpredictable costs for developers. The message is consistent across multiple stories today: the economics of AI are still unstable, and anyone making major infrastructure commitments right now needs to understand what they are actually paying for. Audit before you build. Audit before you buy. That has never been more true than it is today.

Strategic Shifts · Jun 19

SHIFT 1 PREPARE This quarter Regulation

AI Infrastructure Has Crossed Into National Security Territory, and That Changes the Rules for Every Business

Three stories today, read together, describe something that would have seemed far-fetched two years ago. The US government has mandated a fast-track process for AI data centers to connect to the power grid, treating AI compute as a utility on par with defense infrastructure. Separately, the Trump administration is attempting to block a Clean Air Act lawsuit against xAI by arguing that the military needs access to Grok. That is an extraordinary claim: a private AI company's product is now being framed as essential to national defense, insulating it from environmental law.

This builds on a pattern that has been developing all week. Earlier this week, the DOJ intervened on xAI's behalf in the same gas turbine dispute, and SpaceX's $2.6 trillion IPO signaled how tightly the AI and defense-industrial complex are becoming intertwined. When government starts picking winners by granting them regulatory immunity and infrastructure priority, the competitive landscape shifts for everyone downstream. Companies that have relationships with the government-aligned AI infrastructure players will have access advantages. Companies that don't will face compounding disadvantages as power, compute, and regulatory protection concentrate.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

AI vendor selection is no longer just a technology decision. Which AI platforms your organization depends on is now a geopolitical and regulatory risk question.

Operations

Priority grid access for AI data centers means compute infrastructure is going to scale faster than most modeled. Cloud and inference costs could shift significantly in the next 12 months.

What To Do This Week

  • Map your current AI vendor dependencies: which platforms, which models, which cloud infrastructure. Understand your exposure if any one of those players faces regulatory disruption or shifts pricing due to new competitive pressures.
  • Brief your legal and compliance teams on the xAI precedent. The argument that AI tools are military necessities will be tested in court. Understanding how that case resolves will matter for procurement and liability.
  • Do not accelerate infrastructure commitments based on government fast-tracking signals alone. The power grid priority helps hyperscalers, not necessarily your internal AI deployments. Stay focused on what you control.

SHIFT 2 ACT NOW This month Cost compression

The Economics of Frontier AI Are Fracturing, and the Cost Instability Will Hit You Sooner Than You Think

Snap just spun its AI video team out into an independent company called Dotmo. The reason given: the costs of developing frontier AI video were too high to sustain inside a consumer social company. On the same day, Anthropic paused token-based billing for its Claude Agent SDK, a direct signal that the pricing model for agentic AI is not working and needs to be rethought. Baseten is reportedly raising $1.5 billion, just months after a previous mega-round, because inference infrastructure at scale keeps demanding more capital.

These three stories describe the same underlying problem from three different angles. Building AI is expensive and getting more expensive. Pricing AI usage is still an unsolved problem. And the companies closest to the frontier keep needing more money to stay there. For business leaders, this creates a specific operational risk: the AI tools you are paying for today may reprice, restructure, or disappear on short notice. Anthropic pausing billing for a major developer product is not a routine update. It is a company admitting that its monetization model for agents was not fit for purpose. If you have built workflows or budget assumptions around that billing structure, you need to know that.

Who This Affects

Marketing Teams

AI tool costs you budgeted for this year may shift mid-year without much warning. Any vendor using token-based or consumption-based pricing is subject to repricing as companies figure out agentic economics.

Creative Teams

Dotmo launching as an independent company means AI video capabilities that were inside Snap's product pipeline are now on a separate, uncertain funding trajectory. Plan for instability in AI video tooling.

Operations

Consumption-based AI pricing models are becoming unpredictable as agentic use cases scale. Build cost monitoring and caps into every AI deployment before it runs unsupervised.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit every AI tool contract and pricing model your organization is currently using. Identify which vendors are on consumption-based pricing and model what a 2x usage increase would cost. This is not hypothetical: agentic workloads scale non-linearly.
  • If you are building on the Claude Agent SDK, contact your Anthropic rep now to understand the new billing structure before it goes live. Do not wait for the announcement to hit your inbox.
  • For any AI video capability you are counting on in a product roadmap or campaign plan, identify whether that capability sits inside a financially stable platform or a startup that may be mid-fundraise or mid-pivot.

SHIFT 3 MONITOR 12-18 months Competition

The Chip War Is No Longer Just a Geopolitical Story: It Is a Supply Chain Risk for Your AI Roadmap

Two chip stories today, neither of them about Nvidia, tell a larger story when read together. Amazon is preparing to sell its proprietary AI chips externally for the first time, a direct challenge to Nvidia's near-monopoly on AI accelerators. On the same day, the US government is disputing whether ASML's most advanced chip-making equipment has ended up in China, which ASML denies. One story is about new competition entering the hardware market. The other is about whether the global supply chain for the tools needed to make those chips can even be controlled.

For most business leaders, chip supply feels like a problem for hyperscalers, not for them. That framing is wrong. Your AI costs, your model access, and your inference speed are all downstream of chip supply and pricing. If Amazon successfully breaks into external chip sales, Nvidia pricing faces genuine pressure for the first time, and cloud inference costs could come down meaningfully over 18 to 24 months. If the ASML situation escalates into broader export control tightening, the opposite could happen. Both scenarios affect your AI budget. Neither is visible in your current planning.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

AI compute costs are a material line item that will shift based on hardware competition and geopolitical decisions made in the next 12 months. Factor hardware market uncertainty into any multi-year AI investment plans.

Engineering

Amazon entering the external chip market creates a real alternative to Nvidia for inference workloads. Worth tracking whether your cloud provider's Amazon chip offering becomes cost-competitive within the year.

What To Do This Week

  • Do not lock into long-term, fixed-cost AI infrastructure contracts right now. The hardware market is entering a competitive phase that should produce better pricing options within 12 to 18 months.
  • Ask your cloud provider what their roadmap looks like for non-Nvidia inference options. Amazon's move to external sales will accelerate this conversation across all major providers.
  • Flag the ASML situation in your supply chain risk briefings. It is a low-probability, high-impact scenario that deserves a mention even if it does not require action today.

SHIFT 4 PREPARE This quarter Workforce displacement

The Zero-Employee Business Model Is Real, and It Will Reshape How You Think About Headcount and Creative Capacity

The Allbirds CEO launching an AI business with zero employees is easy to read as a novelty story. I think it is one of the most strategically important signals in today's newsletter. It validates something I have been watching closely: the threshold for founding and operating a business is dropping fast, and the first thing to disappear is headcount. Not because people are not valuable, but because AI handles enough of the routine operational and creative work that a single subject-matter expert can now coordinate what used to require a team.

This connects directly to a pattern I flagged earlier this week: only 16% of Americans believe AI will have positive societal impact, and Anthropic has pledged $200 million to study AI's economic impact. The gap between what AI-native founders can build and what the average worker understands about AI is widening every week. For Creative Directors and VPs of Marketing, this is not a distant concern. The agencies and freelancers you use to extend your team's capacity are facing the same structural pressure. A solo operator with strong AI fluency can now deliver what a four-person team delivered two years ago. Your vendors are being disrupted, your internal headcount justifications are being questioned, and the people asking the questions may not understand what they are actually asking.

Who This Affects

Creative Teams

Leadership will increasingly point to stories like the Allbirds CEO's zero-employee startup as justification for headcount pressure. You need a clear articulation of where human creative judgment is irreplaceable and where AI genuinely handles the volume work.

Marketing Teams

Your agency and vendor roster is full of businesses that are being structurally disrupted right now. Evaluate which partners are AI-native and which are applying AI as a layer on top of old operating models.

Leadership/C-Suite

The zero-employee business model signals that AI is compressing the labor component of knowledge work at speed. Any headcount planning that does not account for this is based on an outdated model.

What To Do This Week

  • Before your next agency or vendor review, ask each partner to walk you through their AI-native workflow. What tasks are humans doing that AI could handle? What does their team look like in 18 months if they're honest about it?
  • Run the AI Opportunity Assessment inside your own creative department. Identify the highest-friction tasks in your current process: the ones that consume the most time and deliver the least differentiation. Those are your first automation targets.
  • Have an honest conversation with your leadership team about what AI efficiency gains should actually produce: better work, fewer hours, or both. The default assumption that efficiency gains translate to headcount reductions is the fastest way to destroy team trust and creative quality at the same time.

SHIFT 5 PREPARE This quarter Regulation

Public AI Backlash Has Reached Political Scale: The Regulatory Environment Will Get Messier Before It Gets Clearer

Bernie Sanders proposing $7 trillion in public AI control and 84% of Americans expressing skepticism or negativity about AI's societal impact are not separate stories. They are the same story: the political conditions for aggressive AI regulation are forming faster than the industry expects. Earlier this week, Germany's courts held Google directly liable for AI Overview errors. Anthropic's CEO published a policy essay calling for faster regulation. The Trump administration is shielding xAI from environmental law. These are not coherent signals pointing in one direction. They are a chaotic political environment where AI is simultaneously being accelerated and constrained by different branches of government, different countries, and different political coalitions.

For business leaders, the takeaway is not that Sanders' plan will pass. It almost certainly will not in its current form. The takeaway is that AI has become a major political issue, which means regulatory unpredictability is now a permanent feature of the landscape, not a temporary condition. The analysis arguing that dangerous AI models are coming regardless of policy is correct in one narrow sense: you cannot regulate away capability. But regulation can change liability, data handling requirements, procurement rules, and public sector access overnight. Any organization treating AI regulation as a future problem is already behind.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Regulatory volatility is now a board-level AI risk. Build scenario planning around three regulatory outcomes: status quo, sectoral regulation, and broad liability expansion.

Marketing Teams

Consumer sentiment on AI is negative and hardening. Any customer-facing AI feature needs a human-first framing and a clear opt-out. The Match Group dating data shows what happens to trust when AI feels intrusive.

Operations

Security and data handling practices you have not yet formalized will likely become compliance requirements. The cost of building them proactively is lower than retrofitting under regulatory pressure.

What To Do This Week

  • Conduct a data privacy audit specifically for your AI tools. Most employees do not know that their inputs to AI systems may be used for training. That is a liability and a trust issue, and you need a clear internal policy before a regulator asks for one.
  • Review every customer-facing AI feature or communication for how it is framed. Consumers are increasingly negative on AI. Leading with the benefit, not the technology, is not just good marketing: it is a trust preservation strategy.
  • Assign someone in your organization to track AI regulatory developments quarterly. Not a consultant. An internal owner. The landscape is moving too fast to outsource the awareness.
