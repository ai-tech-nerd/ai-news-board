How today's AI developments apply to your business.

Executive Summary

Three things converged today that, taken together, describe the shape of what's coming faster than most business leaders are prepared for. GM laid off 1,300 workers and installed robots. A tech layoff tracker is now running continuously, with employers explicitly naming AI as the cause. And Fika Jobs just raised $4 million to have AI agents conduct your hiring interviews, meaning the tool that replaces workers is now also screening the workers who replace them. This isn't a future scenario. It's operational today, at scale, inside companies you recognize. The displacement-to-rehiring gap I've been writing about for months is no longer theoretical. It has a TechCrunch tracker and a body count.

At the same time, the infrastructure bets being placed right now are staggering in their size and specificity. Groq confirmed a $650 million raise after Nvidia's $20 billion not-acqui-hire arrangement. SpaceX signed a compute deal with open source AI lab Reflection AI. Google DeepMind committed $75 million directly to A24, one of the most respected film studios in the world. These aren't defensive moves. They're offensive ones, made by organizations that have decided AI is the primary battlefield for the next decade and are allocating accordingly. Meanwhile, Bernie Sanders proposed a $7 trillion public AI plan, and Anthropic may have accidentally triggered its own export ban through its own safety lobbying. The regulatory environment is genuinely chaotic, which means businesses cannot wait for policy clarity before acting.

What this adds up to is a week where the gap between organizations moving and organizations watching just got measurably wider. The companies placing $650 million and $75 million bets are not waiting for the dust to settle. If you're a VP of Marketing or a Creative Director still in evaluation mode, the market is not pausing for your timeline. The practical question right now is not whether to act on AI. It's whether you understand your current processes well enough to know where AI would actually help. That audit-first step is the difference between strategic adoption and expensive noise.

Strategic Shifts · Jun 23

SHIFT 1 ACT NOW This quarter Workforce displacement

The displacement number has a face now, and it's accelerating

For the past year, AI-driven job displacement has been a talking point. Today it became a documented, tracked, named phenomenon. TechCrunch's running 2026 layoff tracker specifically flags employers citing AI as a cause. GM's move, laying off 1,300 workers and installing robots at its flagship EV factory, is the kind of story that moves from tech press to mainstream news, to congressional hearings. These two data points together signal a shift: displacement is no longer deniable or abstract, and the public documentation of it will accelerate the policy and regulatory response.

This connects directly to what I flagged last week when Sanders unveiled his $7 trillion public AI control plan (which appeared in the historical context from June 19 and reappears today). The political infrastructure around AI displacement is forming in real time. Sanders' proposal frames AI as a public utility, which is a direct response to exactly the kind of corporate behavior GM just demonstrated. The policy environment is heating up, and businesses that haven't thought through their own workforce AI strategy are about to find themselves making those decisions under external pressure rather than on their own terms.

The speed problem is the real issue here. Every previous technology wave, computers, the internet, mobile, created displacement followed by a lag and then rehiring. The manufacturing sector had years to adjust. The current wave is compressing that timeline dramatically. When AI coding agents are already teaching robots to install GPUs autonomously (a story that appeared in both today's feed and the June 18 and 19 historical context), the physical and digital labor markets are converging faster than workforce training programs can respond.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Your workforce AI strategy is now a public relations and regulatory risk, not just an operational decision. Companies that moved quietly are about to move visibly, and the optics of layoffs plus robot installations are being tracked publicly and politically.

Operations

The GM model, reduce headcount then automate, is being watched and documented. Operations leaders need a clear internal narrative about how automation decisions are being made and communicated before they become external headlines.

Marketing Teams

The brand risk of AI-driven layoffs is real. Marketing teams need to understand how their organization's AI adoption story is being told internally and whether that story is coherent enough to defend externally.

What To Do This Week

  • Map every role in your organization currently performing repetitive, rules-based tasks and assess which ones are at near-term automation risk. This is not about eliminating roles immediately. It's about knowing your exposure before someone else surfaces it for you.
  • Develop an internal AI workforce policy that distinguishes between AI as an efficiency multiplier for existing employees versus AI as a replacement strategy. The difference matters enormously for retention, morale, and legal exposure.
  • If your organization is considering workforce restructuring alongside AI adoption, get communications leadership and legal involved before the operational decision is finalized, not after.

SHIFT 2 PREPARE This year Enterprise adoption

AI is buying its way into creative industries, and the terms of that entry matter

Google DeepMind's $75 million deal with A24 is not a sponsorship or a technology licensing arrangement. It's a direct investment in creative production pipeline integration with one of the most critically respected studios in the world. A24 makes prestige films. Choosing A24 as the entry point into Hollywood is a deliberate signal that DeepMind is not positioning AI as a cheap content factory. It's positioning AI as a tool for serious creative work. That reframing is strategically significant.

This connects to my belief that Hollywood has already lost its monopoly on entertainment, even if the industry hasn't fully accepted that yet. Netflix should have been contracting AI video creators six months ago. Google DeepMind just accelerated that timeline by funding a respected creative partner who will now have access to AI production tools before their competitors. The $75 million is not really about the films A24 will make. It's about the workflows A24 will prove out, the creative applications that will be validated by prestige work, and the case studies that will then be sold to every other production company watching.

For creative directors and marketing leaders outside Hollywood, this is directly relevant. The workflows being developed inside A24 with DeepMind's backing will become the industry templates. Understanding what those workflows look like, and whether your creative operation is structured to absorb similar tools, is a strategic question you can start answering now. The 'more at-bats' principle applies here: the question is not whether AI will be in your creative process, but whether your team is set up to use additional options productively or whether they'll be overwhelmed by them.

Who This Affects

Creative Teams

The A24 partnership will produce validated AI creative workflows inside a prestige production environment. Those workflows will set expectations for what AI-assisted creative work looks like at a high level, and those expectations will arrive at your door.

Leadership/C-Suite

A $75 million bet by Google DeepMind on creative AI integration is a signal to every CMO and CCO that AI in creative production is moving from experimental to structural. Budget and strategy conversations need to reflect that shift.

Marketing Teams

Video and visual content production timelines and cost assumptions are going to shift as AI-assisted production tools mature through partnerships like this one. Plan for that compression in your 2027 creative budgets.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit your current creative production workflow from brief to final asset. Identify the three stages where the most time is lost or the most rework happens. Those are your highest-value AI integration points, and they're worth knowing before a vendor pitches you a solution.
  • Designate one person on your creative team to track how AI tools are being used in film and high-production video contexts. The techniques being validated at the A24 level will appear in lower-cost tools within 12 to 18 months.
  • If you have a creative department, have a direct conversation with your Creative Director about whether your current briefing and ideation process is structured to take advantage of AI-generated options. If the brief is weak, more options just means more confusion.

SHIFT 3 PREPARE This quarter Workforce displacement

AI hiring is about to screen the candidates AI displaces, and nobody is ready for that conversation

Fika Jobs raised $4 million to build a platform where AI agents conduct job interviews. The timing is extraordinary. On the same day, GM's robot-driven layoffs and the AI displacement tracker are in the news. We now have a documented loop: AI displaces workers, AI restructuring creates new roles, AI agents screen candidates for those new roles. The human is present but reduced at multiple points in the employment cycle simultaneously.

This raises questions that go beyond the obvious bias and fairness concerns (which are real and significant). The deeper issue is that most hiring managers have no framework for evaluating what an AI interview agent actually measures. A video-first AI interview platform assesses verbal responses, cadence, word choice, possibly facial cues. None of that has been validated at scale as a predictor of job performance in knowledge work. Organizations that adopt this without understanding what it's actually selecting for are not just taking on legal risk. They're making foundational talent decisions on unproven criteria.

For the VP of Marketing or Creative Director being asked to fill roles quickly, the appeal is obvious: AI interviews scale. You can screen 200 candidates instead of 20. But the audit-before-adopt principle applies directly here. Before deploying an AI hiring tool, you need to know what your current hiring process actually selects for, what your best performers have in common, and whether an AI interview system can measure those things. Without that baseline, you're not improving your hiring. You're automating a process you don't fully understand.

Who This Affects

Operations

AI interview platforms will be pitched as efficiency solutions for high-volume hiring. Operations leaders need a clear policy on where AI-mediated hiring is appropriate and where human judgment remains required before a vendor conversation happens.

Leadership/C-Suite

AI hiring tools carry legal, ethical, and reputational exposure. Several jurisdictions already require disclosure when AI is used in hiring decisions. Executive leadership needs to be aware before HR adopts these tools at scale.

Marketing Teams

Employer brand is directly affected by candidate experience. An AI-mediated interview process, if poorly implemented or communicated, can damage how your organization is perceived by the talent you most want to attract.

What To Do This Week

  • Before evaluating any AI hiring platform, document what your current hiring process actually measures and which criteria have historically predicted success in the roles you hire for most often. This is the baseline you need to evaluate any alternative.
  • Check the legal requirements in every jurisdiction where you hire regarding AI use in employment decisions. Several states and countries already have disclosure or audit requirements, and enforcement is increasing.
  • If you do pilot an AI interview tool, run it in parallel with your existing process for a defined period and compare outcomes. Do not replace human screening entirely until you have data on whether the AI tool selects for the same qualities your best hires demonstrate.

SHIFT 4 MONITOR 12-18 months Consolidation

The infrastructure layer is consolidating around non-Nvidia players faster than expected

Three infrastructure stories today, taken together, describe a market that is actively hedging against Nvidia dependence. Groq confirmed a $650 million raise following Nvidia's $20 billion not-acqui-hire arrangement. SpaceX signed a compute deal with open source AI lab Reflection AI. Amazon is already selling its own AI chips externally (from the June 19 historical context). The pattern across all three is the same: organizations with significant compute needs or ambitions are making deliberate moves to build or back infrastructure that does not route through Nvidia's pricing and supply decisions.

This matters for business leaders because compute cost is a direct input to every AI product and service you use. If Nvidia maintains pricing power without meaningful competition, the cost of running AI workloads stays high, which keeps AI tool pricing high, which affects your budget. The emergence of credible Nvidia alternatives, whether Groq's inference chips, Amazon's Trainium, or the compute capacity SpaceX is beginning to offer, creates real competitive pressure. That pressure, over 12 to 18 months, should translate to more predictable and potentially lower AI operational costs.

The Ars Technica analysis on the AI bubble's structural vulnerabilities connects here as well. Compute cost is one of the fundamental structural pressures on AI company economics. If inference gets cheaper through competition at the chip layer, some of the margin pressure on AI companies eases. If it doesn't, the bubble analysis gets more compelling. Business leaders don't need to have a position on chip architecture. They do need to understand that their AI vendor pricing is directly tied to this infrastructure battle.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

The infrastructure competition playing out now will directly affect AI tool pricing over the next 12 to 18 months. Locking into long-term AI vendor contracts today without factoring in potential cost compression is a risk worth flagging to your CFO.

Engineering

Engineering teams evaluating AI infrastructure should actively track Groq and alternative inference providers as cost benchmarks, even if Nvidia remains the primary choice. The competitive landscape is shifting quickly enough to revisit quarterly.

Operations

AI operational costs are a variable that will shift as infrastructure competition intensifies. Build cost review cycles into your AI vendor agreements now rather than waiting for contract renewal.

What To Do This Week

  • If you're currently evaluating or renewing AI platform or API contracts, ask vendors directly how their pricing is tied to compute costs and whether pricing scales with infrastructure efficiency gains. This is a negotiating point, not just a technical question.
  • Assign someone to track the Groq and Amazon chip stories quarterly. The goal is not to switch infrastructure providers constantly. It's to have a realistic benchmark for what AI compute should cost so you're not overpaying as competition increases.
  • Review any AI vendor contracts signed in the last 18 months for pricing escalation clauses. If compute gets cheaper and your vendor pricing doesn't adjust, you'll want to know that before renewal.

SHIFT 5 ACT NOW This month Regulation

Agentic AI is creating accountability gaps that no one has solved yet

Two stories today describe the same underlying problem from different directions. The TechCrunch piece on agentic AI feedback loops, systems that operate in recursive cycles without adequate human oversight, is the theoretical framing. The Anthropic export ban story is the real-world consequence: an organization's own public statements, made with good intentions inside a complex regulatory conversation, produced a policy outcome no one intended and that actively harms the organization. Both are stories about systems, whether AI agents or lobbying positions, operating beyond the point where a single human decision-maker can track all the downstream effects.

I've written about human-in-the-loop as fundamentally a communication problem. You set up what you want, you oversee the output, you review the final result. The problem with agentic systems is that the loop is running faster than human review cycles can keep up with. When an AI agent is executing dozens of decisions per minute inside a workflow, the human oversight that validates the system's behavior becomes theoretical rather than actual. The Anthropic situation demonstrates this at the policy level: a sophisticated, well-resourced organization with deep AI expertise still could not fully anticipate how its own communications would be used inside a complex adaptive system (the regulatory environment).

This connects to the security awareness issue I raise consistently. Most organizations deploying AI agents have not defined what 'adequate oversight' actually means in operational terms. They've approved the tool, maybe reviewed initial outputs, and then moved on. That's not oversight. That's abdication with a progress report. The agentic loop risk is not distant. It's present in every automated email sequence, every AI-generated report that goes unreviewed, every workflow where the human has been moved from active participant to occasional auditor.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Agentic AI systems operating in your organization without defined oversight checkpoints create accountability gaps that will eventually surface as operational, legal, or reputational problems. Executive leadership needs to require that every agentic deployment has a defined human review cadence before it goes live.

Marketing Teams

Automated content pipelines, email sequences, and social publishing workflows powered by AI agents need explicit review gates. A feedback loop in a content system can produce and publish outputs at scale before anyone notices they're wrong or off-brand.

Engineering

Engineering teams building or deploying agentic workflows need to design human checkpoint requirements into the architecture from the start, not add them later as a compliance feature. Define what triggers a human review before the system ships.

What To Do This Week

  • Inventory every AI-powered automated workflow currently running in your organization. For each one, identify whether a human is reviewing outputs before they have external impact (customer-facing content, financial decisions, hiring actions). If the answer is no, that's your highest-priority risk item.
  • Define what oversight actually means for each agentic system you operate: how often outputs are reviewed, who is responsible, and what the escalation path is when something is flagged. Vague policies about 'human oversight' are not protection. Specific checkpoints are.
  • Before deploying any new agentic system, require a written description of the feedback loop risk: what happens if the system optimizes for the wrong thing and runs for 48 hours before anyone notices. If your team can't answer that question, the system is not ready to deploy.
