How today's AI developments apply to your business.

Executive Summary

Three things are happening simultaneously right now, and the combination matters more than any single story. First, the companies at the center of AI are racing to own their own infrastructure: OpenAI's custom inference chip with Broadcom, Amazon's $13 billion India commitment, Oracle shedding 21,000 people to fund data centers. This isn't expansion for expansion's sake. It's a structural move to control costs and margins at a level that smaller players simply cannot match. If you're evaluating AI vendors right now, the question of who owns their own compute stack will determine pricing stability and reliability for the next three years.

Second, the displacement story just got a lot harder to look away from. Oracle's 21,000 layoffs funding AI investment and GM installing robots at the exact factory where 1,300 workers were just let go: these aren't abstract workforce trends anymore. They're direct, documented substitutions. I've been watching this pattern build across the past week, and what concerns me most isn't the displacement itself. It's the speed. There is no smooth transition window. The businesses funding that displacement are the same ones selling you the AI tools to become more efficient. That's not cynicism. It's just the math you need to understand before you make your next hiring decision.

Third, and this one is for my VP of Marketing and Creative Director readers specifically: Adobe acquiring Topaz Labs tells you something important about where the creative tool stack is heading. The consolidation of best-in-class AI enhancement capabilities into platforms you already use is accelerating. Before you approve budget for another standalone AI creative tool, you should know what's already coming to the tools you're already paying for. The audit-before-you-adopt principle has never been more relevant than in a quarter when Adobe, Microsoft, and Google are all absorbing the standalone tools that marketing and creative teams adopted two years ago.

Strategic Shifts · Jun 25

SHIFT 1 PREPARE This quarter Consolidation

The infrastructure layer is being privatized by the companies that need it most

OpenAI and Broadcom's joint custom LLM inference chip announcement, sitting right next to Amazon's $13 billion India infrastructure commitment and Oracle's 21,000-person layoff to fund AI data centers, reveals a pattern that goes beyond competition. The largest AI consumers are methodically eliminating their dependency on third-party infrastructure. OpenAI's chip is designed specifically to reduce reliance on Nvidia. Amazon is building capacity in a market where enterprise AI demand is outpacing available compute. Oracle is making a calculated bet that the cost of human capital is better deployed as physical infrastructure.

This connects directly to last week's stories. On June 24, OpenAI's Broadcom chip was first reported. On June 19, Amazon announced it would sell its proprietary AI chips externally, challenging Nvidia. The week before saw SpaceX acquiring Cursor and Baseten raising $1.5 billion for AI inference. What looked like separate funding stories is actually a coordinated infrastructure land grab. The companies building AI are trying to own the entire stack: model, chip, data center, and cloud. When the infrastructure layer consolidates at this speed, pricing power and access become strategic weapons.

For business leaders, this means the AI vendor landscape will look very different in 18 months. Companies that own their compute will have structural cost advantages that let them undercut or outperform vendors who don't. If you're signing multi-year AI service contracts right now without understanding your vendor's infrastructure position, you're making a significant blind bet.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Vendor selection and pricing stability are tied directly to infrastructure ownership. A vendor without its own compute stack is subject to Nvidia pricing, which compresses margins and creates unpredictable cost structures that flow downstream to you.

Engineering

Custom silicon for LLM inference means performance benchmarks will shift quickly. The inference speeds and cost-per-token your team is modeling today may look very different by year-end as proprietary chips come online.

Operations

Any AI tools your organization is running at scale need a vendor stability assessment. If your provider doesn't own its infrastructure, understand what happens to your contract if their compute costs spike.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit your current AI vendor contracts for compute dependency clauses: understand whether your pricing is fixed or variable, and whether your vendor owns its own infrastructure.
  • Add infrastructure ownership as a vendor evaluation criterion in your next procurement cycle. It belongs alongside security, SLA, and pricing.
  • Map your AI tool spend against vendors who are vertically integrated versus those dependent on third-party compute. The map will tell you where your pricing risk lives.

SHIFT 2 ACT NOW This quarter Workforce displacement

The displacement-to-layoff equation is now direct and documented, not theoretical

Oracle cut 21,000 people and is using the savings to fund AI infrastructure. GM laid off 1,300 workers and installed robots at the same factory. These aren't two unrelated stories. They're the same story happening at different scales, and the connection between the cuts and the automation investment is no longer implied. Companies are saying it plainly: we are replacing human cost with AI and robotics investment.

I've been thinking about the displacement-to-rehiring gap for a long time, and what these stories confirm is that the gap is now visible in quarterly earnings cycles, not historical retrospect. The former Infosys chief launching a startup to challenge the traditional IT services model reinforces this from another angle: the people who built the legacy systems know those systems are already obsolete. They're not defending the old model. They're building the replacement.

The data point on engineering jobs being the most resilient to AI displacement is genuinely useful context here. It suggests the displacement is not uniform. Repetitive and process-oriented roles are going first, and roles requiring judgment, system design, and domain expertise are holding. This is the pattern I'd expect, and it has real implications for how you structure your team right now. The question isn't whether AI will affect your headcount. It's whether you're building a team that's positioned on the right side of that divide.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Workforce planning decisions made in the next two quarters will be extremely difficult to reverse. Understanding which roles in your organization are process-oriented versus judgment-oriented is essential before your next budget cycle.

Operations

The GM and Oracle stories show that automation ROI is being calculated at the board level and used to justify headcount decisions. Operations leaders need to know which of their processes are already automatable so they can lead that conversation rather than have it happen to them.

Marketing Teams

Marketing roles built around repetitive content production, reporting, and scheduling are the most exposed. Roles built around strategy, brand judgment, and client relationships are the most resilient. Know which category your team sits in.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit your team's current workflow by role and categorize tasks as process-repetitive versus judgment-dependent. This is the first honest step toward understanding where AI augments and where it replaces.
  • Identify which roles in your department could be restructured around AI oversight rather than AI replacement: one expert overseeing automated systems is a sustainable model; wholesale elimination without a plan is not.
  • Before your next headcount conversation, document what your team actually does at the task level, not the job title level. Job titles are already obsolete as displacement metrics.

SHIFT 3 PREPARE This month Consolidation

Creative tool consolidation is accelerating: the standalone AI tools you bought are being absorbed

Adobe acquiring Topaz Labs is a signal, not just a transaction. Topaz built some of the most respected AI-powered image and video enhancement tools in the professional creative market: upscaling, noise reduction, sharpening. These were tools that creative professionals adopted precisely because Adobe didn't offer them yet. Now Adobe owns them. This is the same pattern we saw when Adobe absorbed Firefly capabilities, when Canva absorbed AI generation features, when Microsoft pushed Copilot into every product it owned.

The strategic message is clear. The AI tool ecosystem is collapsing into platforms. The standalone tools that creative teams adopted in 2023 and 2024 as workarounds are being acquired and folded into subscriptions you're already paying for. This creates a specific trap for marketing and creative leaders: you may be paying for redundant tools without knowing it, because the audit never happened.

I built the Creative Brief Builder as a standalone tool and sold it to AAA as a first client. I understand the appeal of purpose-built tools. But what I watch carefully is whether the platform your organization already lives in is about to offer the same capability. For creative teams, the Adobe Creative Cloud ecosystem absorbing Topaz means you need to reassess your current AI enhancement tool spend before the next subscription renewal cycle. The capability is coming to you. The question is whether you'll notice before you auto-renew something redundant.

Who This Affects

Creative Teams

The AI enhancement tools you use outside of Adobe, particularly for upscaling and video sharpening, may become redundant as Topaz Labs capabilities integrate into Creative Cloud. Review your current tools before your next renewal.

Marketing Teams

Marketing teams that built workflows around standalone AI creative tools need to track platform consolidation closely. Paying for three tools that do what one platform subscription now covers is a budget and workflow problem.

Operations

SaaS tool audits should now include a consolidation flag: tracking which standalone AI tools are at acquisition risk or being absorbed by platforms the organization already subscribes to.

What To Do This Week

  • Run an immediate audit of every AI creative tool your team is paying for as a standalone subscription and compare it against the current and announced feature roadmaps of the platforms you already use, specifically Adobe Creative Cloud.
  • Flag every tool renewal coming up in the next 90 days and check whether the core capability is now available in a platform you already own before approving renewal.
  • For Creative Directors under pressure to show AI ROI: this audit is your fastest win. Eliminating redundant tool spend while demonstrating platform utilization is a concrete, defensible ROI story you can bring to leadership this quarter.

SHIFT 4 MONITOR This quarter Regulation

AI policy advocacy is producing unintended consequences that affect enterprise access

The Anthropic export ban story is genuinely strange and important. Anthropic, one of the most safety-focused AI labs, may have contributed to US government decisions that now restrict Anthropic's own international operations. Meanwhile, Europe is actively pushing back against US chip export controls, viewing them as a threat to European AI competitiveness. These two stories together reveal a policy environment where no actor, not even the companies most engaged in shaping it, can predict the outcomes.

This connects to a pattern building over the past week. On June 22, the Trump administration's crackdown on Anthropic was first reported. On the same day, a historical analysis of failed export controls ran, arguing that technology restrictions have consistently failed to achieve their goals. The geopolitical dimension is fracturing further as Europe resists aligning with US semiconductor policy. What looked like a US-China technology conflict is becoming a three-way tension between US companies, US policy, and US allies.

For enterprise leaders, this matters in a specific way: if you are evaluating AI vendors with international operations or building AI capabilities that rely on chips with export restrictions, your technology roadmap now has geopolitical risk baked in. That's not a reason to stop. It's a reason to know which of your AI dependencies cross international lines and what your contingency looks like if access changes.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Enterprise AI vendor risk now includes geopolitical exposure. Any vendor operating across US-restricted markets or dependent on chip supply chains subject to export controls carries risk that belongs in your technology risk register.

Engineering

Teams building AI pipelines that rely on specific hardware or model providers should understand whether those dependencies are subject to export restrictions, particularly if your organization operates in international markets.

What To Do This Week

  • Add a geopolitical dependency column to your AI vendor assessment: flag any vendor with significant operations in restricted markets or hardware dependencies subject to export controls.
  • If your organization operates internationally, ask your AI vendors directly which of their capabilities are subject to export restrictions and how a restriction change would affect your service agreement.
  • Watch the Europe-US semiconductor tension closely over the next quarter. A fracture in Western chip policy alignment would affect pricing and availability for the entire enterprise AI stack.

SHIFT 5 MONITOR This year Cost compression

The AI bubble skepticism narrative is gaining structural credibility, not just volume

An Ars Technica analysis identifying structural vulnerabilities in the AI investment cycle sits alongside Cerebras' stock plunge after an earnings miss and a $7 trillion public control proposal from Bernie Sanders. Individually, each of these is easy to dismiss: one is an opinion piece, one is a volatile stock in a volatile sector, and one is a political proposal with near-zero passage probability. Together, they represent something worth watching: the skeptical case for AI investment sustainability is starting to build evidence, not just rhetoric.

This trend connects to June 24's analysis of the same Ars Technica piece, which appeared in last week's historical context. The fact that substantive bubble analysis is running in technical publications alongside actual earnings misses from public AI hardware companies is a different signal than the venture capital euphoria that dominated coverage six months ago. Cerebras' CEO claiming the market misunderstood the margin outlook is not reassuring. That's the explanation companies give when the numbers didn't match the story.

I'm not predicting an AI bubble burst, and I'd push back hard on anyone who thinks slowing investment means the technology stops developing. The infrastructure buildout has too much committed capital and too many real enterprise contracts behind it. But there is a meaningful difference between AI as a technology transformation and AI companies as investments. Some of the companies getting billion-dollar valuations today will not exist in three years. For enterprise buyers, vendor stability assessment isn't just good practice. It's risk management.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Vendor viability is a real consideration in a market where AI companies are burning capital faster than they're generating sustainable revenue. Multi-year commitments to AI vendors without financial stability assessment carry real contract risk.

Operations

Operational dependencies on AI tools from companies without clear paths to profitability create continuity risk. Know which of your critical AI tools come from companies still pre-revenue or burning through funding rounds.

What To Do This Week

  • For any AI vendor you're considering a multi-year contract with, check their funding runway and whether they've demonstrated a path to profitability, not just impressive ARR growth on a small base.
  • Maintain parallel capability awareness: for any critical AI function, know which two or three vendors could replace your current provider if it fails or gets acquired. Consolidation and failure are both real outcomes.
  • Don't let bubble skepticism paralyze action. The right response to uncertainty is optionality, not paralysis: shorter contract terms, modular integrations, and documented vendor exit plans.
