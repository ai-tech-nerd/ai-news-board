How today's AI developments apply to your business.

Executive Summary

Two things crystallized for me reading today's stories together. First: the AI infrastructure war is no longer about who has the best model. It's about who controls the full stack, from the silicon up. OpenAI and Broadcom launched a custom LLM inference chip. IBM claims a sub-1 nanometer semiconductor breakthrough. Amazon dropped another $13 billion into India's AI infrastructure. Oracle laid off 21,000 people and pointed the savings directly at compute investment. These aren't isolated bets. They're the same bet, placed by multiple players simultaneously: the companies that own their own hardware will have structural cost and speed advantages that companies renting general-purpose GPUs simply cannot match. That pattern has been building all week, and today it solidified.

Second: AI agents are no longer theoretical. Notion killed its email app because users switched to AI agents for email management. GM replaced 1,300 workers with robots. Patronus AI raised $50 million specifically to stress-test agents before deployment, which only makes sense if enterprises are actually deploying agents at scale. General Intuition raised $2.3 billion to train agents in video game environments. The agent economy isn't coming. It's here, and it's starting to eat existing software categories whole.

For the VP of Marketing or Creative Director reading this: the pressure you feel right now is real, and it's going to increase. The displacement-to-rehiring gap I keep coming back to is widening. Businesses are redirecting human labor costs into AI capital at a pace that should concern anyone who hasn't already audited where AI fits in their own operation. Not because they need to panic, but because the window to make thoughtful decisions is shorter than it looks.

Strategic Shifts · Jun 26

SHIFT 1 PREPARE This year Vertical integration and cost compression

The AI stack war has moved from models to silicon, and most businesses are flying blind on what that means for their costs

OpenAI and Broadcom launched a chip designed specifically for LLM inference. IBM claims a sub-1 nanometer process node. Amazon committed $13 billion to India AI infrastructure. These aren't stories about raw AI capability. They're stories about who will control the cost of running AI at scale, and they connect directly to Oracle's decision to lay off 21,000 people and plow the savings into infrastructure debt. The pattern I've been watching build all week is now unmistakable: the companies that vertically integrate their AI stack, from chip design through model training through inference, will be able to offer AI capabilities at a fraction of what it costs to run on general-purpose Nvidia GPUs.

This matters to business leaders for a reason that isn't obvious at first glance. Right now, AI pricing for enterprise tools looks relatively stable. Behind that stability is a cost structure that's about to shift dramatically. When OpenAI's inference costs drop because they're running on their own silicon, they can either compress margins further to win market share or improve model quality at the same price point. Either outcome changes the competitive landscape for every AI vendor your company is currently paying. The tools you're evaluating today may be priced and positioned very differently in 18 months.

The chip story connects to the export control friction also visible today, where Europe is pushing back on Washington's semiconductor policies. The geopolitical dimension here is real. If chip supply chains fracture further, the companies that have already secured their own silicon supply, like OpenAI with Broadcom, will have a meaningful advantage over those still dependent on Nvidia or TSMC allocation.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Your AI vendor costs and capabilities are about to shift as infrastructure economics change. The tools you're standardizing on today may look very different in 18 months. Lock-in risk is real.

Operations

If your team is running AI workloads directly on cloud infrastructure rather than through managed APIs, the cost structure of your compute spend is about to be disrupted. Worth a line item review.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit every AI vendor contract for lock-in clauses before the cost compression wave hits. If you're on annual or multi-year terms, understand what flexibility you have.
  • Map which of your AI capabilities are inference-heavy versus training-heavy. Inference cost drops are coming fastest, which affects chatbots, content generation tools, and customer-facing AI features first.
  • Track whether your primary AI vendors are building their own silicon or staying Nvidia-dependent. That single factor will increasingly predict their pricing trajectory.

SHIFT 2 ACT NOW This quarter Enterprise adoption and workflow displacement

AI agents are eating software categories, and the first casualties are the productivity tools your teams use every day

Notion killed its email app. The stated reason: most users switched to AI agents for email management. That sentence deserves a second read. A venture-backed company built a product, shipped it, acquired users, and then shut it down because AI agents made the product category obsolete. That's not a future-tense story. It happened. And it happened fast enough to kill a live product.

Paired with Patronus AI raising $50 million to build stress-testing environments for AI agents before deployment, the signal is clear: enterprise agent deployment is accelerating fast enough that there's now a market for pre-deployment safety infrastructure. You don't raise $50 million to solve a theoretical problem. Meanwhile, General Intuition raised $2.3 billion on the thesis that video game simulations are the best training ground for real-world AI agents. These are not small bets. They reflect serious conviction that agents are moving from demos to deployments at scale.

For marketing and creative teams, the question isn't whether agents will affect your workflows. They already are, quietly, inside the tools you use. The question is which of your current software subscriptions are quietly becoming redundant as the tools you actually rely on absorb agent capabilities. This is exactly where the audit-before-adopt principle pays off: if you don't have a clear inventory of what tools your team uses and why, you won't notice when a tool becomes obsolete until you've already renewed the contract.

Who This Affects

Marketing Teams

Marketing automation platforms are actively pivoting to agent-first architectures. MoEngage made this explicit last week. The tools you use for email, social scheduling, and campaign management are being rebuilt around agents. Your workflows will change whether you plan for it or not.

Operations

Productivity software consolidation is accelerating. Tools that exist to organize, route, or process information are the most vulnerable to agent displacement. Audit your software stack now for redundancies before your next renewal cycle.

Creative Teams

Creative project management and briefing tools are in the same category as email apps: organizational layers that agents can absorb. The upside is fewer context-switching costs. The risk is losing tools before replacements are ready.

What To Do This Week

  • Run a software audit specifically looking for tools whose primary job is organizing, routing, or surfacing information. Those are the categories agents replace first. Flag anything with an upcoming renewal.
  • Ask your team which tools they actually use daily versus which ones they technically have access to. Notion killing its email app is a reminder that adoption gaps are real, and you may be paying for tools no one is using because agents already filled the gap informally.
  • Before renewing any productivity or marketing automation subscription, ask the vendor directly: what is your agent roadmap, and how does this tool evolve in an agentic workflow? Their answer will tell you a lot about whether this vendor has a future.

SHIFT 3 PREPARE This quarter Regulation and geopolitical fracture

The AI competition is fracturing along geopolitical lines, and the collateral damage lands on enterprise security and vendor trust

Anthropic accused Alibaba of orchestrating the largest Claude cloning attack ever recorded, claiming Alibaba defied Trump administration directives in the process. The White House separately asked OpenAI to delay a model release over safety concerns. Europe is formally pushing back on US chip export controls. These three stories in one day aren't coincidence. They're the visible surface of a deeper fracture in how AI is developed, deployed, and governed across national boundaries.

The Alibaba story in particular deserves attention from anyone running enterprise AI. If Anthropic's most capable model can be systematically cloned at this scale, it means the competitive moat around frontier AI is thinner than anyone wants to admit. It also means that the AI tools you're evaluating for enterprise use are operating in a threat environment where capability theft is happening at an industrial level. This connects directly to the security belief I come back to repeatedly: most businesses have no idea what security risks they're actually carrying with their AI tooling. The Alibaba story makes that risk concrete.

The White House intervention in OpenAI's release schedule is a different signal. Government oversight of frontier model releases has been discussed for years as a theoretical future. Today it happened. That's not a reason to slow down enterprise AI adoption, but it is a reason to build your AI strategy around vendors who can demonstrate governance maturity, not just capability.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Vendor trust and geopolitical risk are now real factors in AI procurement decisions. If your AI vendor's core technology is the subject of state-level intellectual property disputes, that's a supply chain risk, not just a news story.

Operations

The data your teams put into AI systems crosses borders and jurisdictions in ways most companies don't fully understand. A formal review of what data flows through which AI tools, and under what terms, is no longer optional.

Engineering

If your team is evaluating open source or less-established AI models for cost reasons, the Alibaba story raises a direct question: do you know the provenance of the model you're using? Cloned models carry inherited risk.

What To Do This Week

  • Audit which AI vendors your teams are using and where those companies are headquartered, funded, and subject to legal jurisdiction. This isn't paranoia; it's basic vendor risk management that most companies haven't applied to AI tools yet.
  • Review your AI vendor terms specifically for data training opt-out provisions. Most people don't know that by default, inputs to many AI systems are used for model training. Turning that off is often a setting, not a negotiation, but you have to know to look for it.
  • Add AI vendor governance questions to your next procurement checklist: How does this vendor handle government requests? What is their policy on model deployment delays? Have they had any security incidents involving model theft or data exposure?

SHIFT 4 ACT NOW This quarter Workforce displacement

Human labor is being converted into AI capital at a pace businesses need to acknowledge honestly, not euphemistically

Oracle laid off 21,000 people and redirected the cost savings into AI infrastructure investment. GM installed robots at its flagship EV factory after laying off 1,300 workers. TechCrunch's running layoff tracker, which has been active all week, continues to grow with companies explicitly citing AI as the reason. These aren't separate stories. They're one story told in different industries simultaneously.

I've written before about the displacement-to-rehiring gap, and today's stories are a clean illustration of why that gap concerns me more than any individual layoff number. The issue isn't that Oracle or GM made bad decisions for their businesses. The issue is that the jobs eliminated today don't have clear successors yet. The new roles that AI creates, AI infrastructure specialists, agent deployment managers, model governance leads, require skills that take time to develop and workers to transition. The gap between when displacement happens and when rehiring aligns with the new landscape is exactly where people fall through.

For the business leaders reading this: I'm not saying don't adopt AI. The competitive pressure is real and the efficiency gains are real. But if your AI strategy is just a cost reduction strategy with AI as the justification, you're going to have workforce morale, talent pipeline, and institutional knowledge problems that don't show up in the Q3 numbers but will hit you hard in 18 months. The companies that navigate this best will be explicit with their teams about what's changing and why, and will invest in transition support rather than just transition announcements.

Who This Affects

Leadership/C-Suite

Workforce displacement framed purely as efficiency gain creates trust deficits that compound over time. The companies winning the talent war for the new AI-adjacent roles are the ones that handled the displacement phase with transparency.

Marketing Teams

Marketing is typically an early target for AI-driven headcount reduction because so much of what marketing teams do is content creation and distribution, both of which AI handles well. If your team doesn't know where they stand, that uncertainty is already affecting productivity.

Operations

When labor cost savings are redirected to AI capital, operations teams are often the ones managing both the transition and the new systems simultaneously. Make sure the efficiency gains are actually creating capacity, not just shifting pressure.

What To Do This Week

  • If you're a leader whose team is using AI tools to increase output, have a direct conversation about what that increased capacity means for workload expectations. AI efficiency should translate to better work-life balance and higher quality output, not simply more volume from the same headcount.
  • Identify which roles in your organization are most exposed to AI displacement in the next 12 months and start having honest conversations now, not when a decision is already made. People who feel informed are more likely to adapt than people who feel blindsided.
  • Map the new AI-adjacent roles your organization will need: someone to oversee your agent deployments, someone to manage your knowledge base and data quality, someone to evaluate AI tool ROI. These are the roles worth investing in now, and they're best filled by people who already know your business.

SHIFT 5 PREPARE This quarter Consolidation

Adobe's Topaz acquisition signals creative tool consolidation is accelerating, and the independent AI tool market for creatives is shrinking

Adobe acquiring Topaz Labs is a smaller story in today's lineup, but strategically it's one of the more consequential ones for creative teams. Topaz Labs built a loyal following among photographers and videographers specifically because its tools did things Adobe's native toolset didn't do as well: aggressive upscaling, noise reduction, and video enhancement that professionals actually preferred over Premiere and Lightroom's built-in options. Adobe buying Topaz removes a best-in-class independent option and consolidates it into Creative Cloud.

The pattern here connects to what I think of as the creative tool audit imperative. If your team is using Topaz Labs today, their workflow is about to change. If your team doesn't know whether they're using Topaz Labs, that's an audit gap. But the larger point is that Adobe has been systematically acquiring the AI tools that creative professionals were using as Creative Cloud alternatives or supplements: Firefly for generative image, Frame.io for video collaboration, and now Topaz for enhancement. The independent creative AI tool market that flourished over the last three years is consolidating fast.

For creative directors specifically, this is both a risk and an opportunity. The risk: vendor lock-in deepens as Adobe absorbs the alternatives. The opportunity: if Adobe integrates Topaz well, the upscaling and enhancement workflows your team currently does as a separate step could become native to the tools they already use. More at-bats in the same time, which is the right way to think about AI's value for creative teams. But that only works if someone is actually tracking the workflow and measuring what changes.

Who This Affects

Creative Teams

If Topaz Labs tools are part of your current workflow, plan for integration changes and potential pricing shifts as Adobe absorbs the product. Best to document your current enhancement workflow now so you can measure whether Adobe's integration actually improves or degrades it.

Operations

Creative software licensing is consolidating into fewer, larger vendors. This simplifies procurement but increases switching costs and lock-in risk. Factor this into your next software audit.

What To Do This Week

  • Survey your creative team right now on which tools they're using for image and video enhancement outside of native Adobe tools. You need to know this before Adobe's integration roadmap changes those tools.
  • If your team relies on Topaz Labs for specific quality outputs, document those outputs as benchmarks now. When Adobe integrates the technology, you'll need a baseline to evaluate whether the integration maintains the quality your team depends on.
  • Use this acquisition as a prompt to review your full creative software stack for consolidation exposure. Which of your current tools are independent and potentially acquisition targets? Where does your team have workflow dependencies on tools that could change hands?

OpenAI x Broadcom LLM Inference Chip

Track this as a pricing indicator, not just a hardware story.
